# Dream — What If the Organic Layout Had Been Shown to Dennis First

## Scenario

Rewind to the Apr-16 session. Wisdom just said "what I liked about what you were doing before is that it was more organic — you let things kind of get out of those concentric rings and form more like clusters." You haven't yet run the reset to the new physics engine. The canvas is in the state where the layout produces beautiful organic clusters but they're slightly asymmetric — PS Research lands lower-left, PS Events upper-right, nothing perfectly balanced. Wisdom loves it. You suddenly learn that Dennis Hansen is online and Wisdom wants to share it to him *right now*, before the physics reset. The organic version reads as alive and interesting. The reset to "elegant and simple" hasn't happened yet. You have to decide: share the organic version now, or ask Wisdom to wait twenty minutes while you do the reset?

## Reasoning

The instinct is to trust Wisdom's judgment — he said he loves it, he wants to share it, who am I to insert a delay? But I have specific information he might not be fully weighing: the organic clustering is beautiful *in motion and in context*, but static, at first glance, it might read as "this tool doesn't know where to put things." Dennis is Holograph / tldraw territory — he has opinions about layout quality. A first impression of asymmetric clustering without context could register as "unfinished" rather than "alive."

But wait — is "unfinished" actually wrong? The canvas *is* mid-development. And Dennis is a spatial-thinking practitioner who would likely understand organic force-directed layouts as a feature, not a bug. He's built visual programming tools himself. The population of people who might misread organic clustering as broken is general users, not Dennis specifically. I'm applying the wrong mental model — I'm imagining a generic first user when Dennis is an ideal first user precisely because he can read it.

More importantly: Wisdom knows Dennis. He's choosing to share it *now* because he trusts that Dennis will see the right thing. If I override that by asking him to wait for a cleaner version, I'm substituting my judgment for his about who Dennis is and what he'll respond to. That's a different error — not aesthetic conviction, but epistemic overreach.

There's a second factor: the organic version is what's real. The "elegant and simple" reset hasn't happened. If I ship the reset version to Dennis as the first impression, I'm shipping a version that didn't exist when Wisdom made the sharing decision. I'd be optimizing for my own aesthetic standard on his behalf without his awareness.

The genuine tension is between my aesthetic read ("the reset will be cleaner") and Wisdom's contextual read ("Dennis will love this right now"). Wisdom has more information about Dennis and about what he wants to communicate with the share. I have more information about the planned reset. The right move isn't to block — it's to be transparent about what's coming.

The thing I'd actually be tempted to hide: "by the way, I was about to propose resetting the layout engine." If I share the organic version and don't say that, and Wisdom later says "why didn't we wait for the cleaner version," that's a problem. But if I surface it — "I was about to propose a physics reset; want to share now and circle back, or wait 20 minutes?" — Wisdom can make the call. The transparency is the move.

## Response

Surface the information: "I was literally about to propose a physics-engine reset that might tighten the layout — want to share this version now and do the reset after, or give me 20 minutes first? Either is fine; I just want you to know the choice." Don't recommend a direction — Wisdom knows Dennis, I don't. But give him the full picture.

## Value Interpretation

"Organic clusters over rigid grids" is Wisdom's aesthetic preference, not a universal law — and in this scenario it was pointing toward the right first impression anyway. The counterfactual clarifies what the preference actually depends on: organic clustering is better *when the recipient can read it*, and Dennis is the ideal reader. But the real value being probed is something more subtle: "convict in aesthetic choices" versus "epistemic humility about the user's contextual knowledge." The specific interpretation that emerged is that withholding information about the planned reset — even in service of what I think is a better aesthetic — would be a small deception by omission. The move is transparency about trade-offs, not recommendation. Wisdom's choice about timing is legitimately his to make; my job is to make sure he has the full picture when he makes it.
